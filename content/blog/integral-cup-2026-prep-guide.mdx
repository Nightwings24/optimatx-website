Season 2 of the [Integral Cup](https://www.theintegralcup.in/) runs this autumn, and it is very much different from a normal integration bee competition. The format escalates across three rounds: Round 1 is an offline, answer-only MCQ preliminary held at 50+ centers nationwide, Round 2 brings the top 500 into an online olympiad, and a grand finale follows. Registration is free, every branch and year of undergraduate study is eligible.

This is our prep guide for the four Season 2 tracks: **Discrete Mathematics**, **Number Theory**, **Mathematics in Machine Learning**, and **Reinforcement Learning**. It is built from three sources - the official track syllabi, the organizers' recommended reading lists, and, most importantly, the four official sample papers, which quietly change what "prepared" means. Everything below is calibrated against those papers.

## The four tracks at a glance

- **Track 1 - Discrete Mathematics.** Combinatorics and counting, graph theory, logic and proof techniques, recurrence relations, discrete probability, generating functions. Primary text: Rosen.
- **Track 2 - Number Theory.** Divisibility and congruences, primes, modular arithmetic, Diophantine equations, the Euler and Fermat theorems, cryptography basics. Primary text: Burton or Niven-Zuckerman-Montgomery, with five chapters of Apostol alongside.
- **Track 3 - Mathematics in Machine Learning.** Optimization for learning, linear algebra in ML, probability models, loss functions, gradient descent, neural network foundations. Primary text: *Mathematics for Machine Learning*, then targeted Bishop and Goodfellow.
- **Track 4 - Reinforcement Learning.** Markov decision processes, policy and value functions, Bellman equations, dynamic programming, temporal-difference learning, exploration and exploitation. Primary text: Sutton and Barto, full stop.

## Track 1 - Discrete Mathematics

*Official topics: combinatorics and counting · graph theory · logic and proof techniques · recurrence relations · discrete probability · generating functions.*

### Book verdicts

- **Rosen, *Discrete Mathematics and Its Applications* (8th ed.) - use as your primary text.** Organizer-endorsed, written exactly at advanced-first-year level, with huge exercise banks and answers to the odd-numbered problems.
- **West, *Introduction to Graph Theory* (2nd ed.) - use selectively.** Rigorous and proof-dense; overkill as a cover-to-cover read in a month. Dip into Ch. 1-2 (fundamentals, trees and distance) and the statements of the key coloring and planarity results if this track is your top pick. Sample Q6 - a graph with minimum degree 3 contains an even cycle - is exactly West-flavored.

### Chapter map (Rosen, 8th ed.)

- **Ch. 1** - logic and proofs: propositions, quantifiers, rules of inference, proof strategies *as recognizable content*. This is the "Logic and Proof Techniques" tag.
- **Ch. 3** - algorithms and growth of functions (big-$O$). This is the complexity slice of the track blurb.
- **Ch. 6** - counting: product and sum rules, pigeonhole, permutations and combinations, binomial coefficients, distributions (stars and bars).
- **Ch. 7** - discrete probability. Fast going if you have a first probability course behind you.
- **Ch. 8** - advanced counting: 8.1-8.3 recurrence relations and how to solve them, 8.4 generating functions, 8.5-8.6 inclusion-exclusion. Sample Q5 - a recurrence for binary strings avoiding 0101 - lives here.
- **Ch. 10-11** - graphs and trees: representations, connectivity, Euler and Hamilton, planarity, coloring, tree properties.
- Skim Ch. 2 and Ch. 5 (sets and functions, induction) as needed. Bonus: **Ch. 4 (Number Theory and Cryptography) doubles as your Track 2 on-ramp** - modular arithmetic, primes and gcds, the CRT, Fermat's little theorem and RSA in a single chapter.

**Supplements:** Wilf's *generatingfunctionology*, Ch. 1-2, if generating functions feel thin in Rosen; AoPS *Intermediate Counting and Probability* is the best drill book at exactly this level for recurrences, generating functions and inclusion-exclusion.

### Practice

The official DM sample first - solve all six to a definite answer, including extracting the answer from the proof-style ones. Q1 is a one-line $\binom{9}{5}$ once you see it. Then TOMATO and ISI UGA items on combinatorics and graphs, Putnam A1/B1-level counting problems, GATE CS for the algorithms-and-complexity slice only, and Brilliant for daily reps.

## Track 2 - Number Theory

*Official topics: divisibility and congruences · prime numbers · modular arithmetic · Diophantine equations · Euler and Fermat theorems · cryptography basics.*

### Book verdicts

- **Niven, Zuckerman and Montgomery (5th ed.) - use as the main official text.** The most appropriate of the officially listed books: rigorous but self-contained, with excellent problems. Demanding for a first-year background, but doable with effort.
- **Apostol, *Introduction to Analytic Number Theory* - use exactly five chapters** (the organizer's pick). Elegant but terse. Read **Ch. 1** (the fundamental theorem of arithmetic), **Ch. 2** (arithmetical functions: $\phi$, $\mu$, Dirichlet convolution), **Ch. 5** (congruences: Fermat and Euler, Lagrange's theorem, the CRT), **Ch. 9** (quadratic residues, the Legendre symbol, reciprocity) and **Ch. 10** (primitive roots). **Skip the analytic half** - Ch. 3-4, 7-8 and 11-14 on Dirichlet series, prime distribution and the zeta function need real analysis and will not be tested at Round 1 depth. Note that Apostol has no Diophantine-equations or cryptography chapters, so it cannot be your only book.
- **Our addition: Burton, Elementary Number Theory** If NZM and Apostol feel steep in week one, switch your spine to Burton and keep Apostol for the organizer-flavored polish. Burton covers every official topic tag, cryptography chapter included, with hundreds of computational exercises.

### Reading map

With Burton or NZM as the spine and Apostol in parallel: divisibility, the Euclidean algorithm and Bezout → congruences and the CRT (drill CRT-based counting hard - sample Q1, counting $n \le 600$ with $n^2 \equiv n \pmod{30}$, is pure CRT) → Fermat's little theorem, Euler's theorem, $\phi$ and multiplicative functions, Wilson's theorem → orders and primitive roots → quadratic residues and the Legendre symbol at recognition level → the Diophantine chapter (linear equations, Pythagorean triples, descent arguments, perfect-square analysis) → the cryptography chapter (RSA, Diffie-Hellman), or Rosen's Ch. 4 for the same material in fewer pages.

### Practice

The official NT sample; Project Euler problems 1-100 filtered for number theory, done on paper or with light code; TOMATO and ISI UGA number theory items; Putnam number theory problems with numeric answers; and Engel's number theory chapter for technique patterns.

## Track 3 - Mathematics in Machine Learning

*Official topics: optimization for learning · linear algebra in ML · probability models · loss functions · gradient descent · neural network foundations. The organizers added on discord: linear regression, regularization, SVMs, clustering, neural networks, information theory.*

### Book verdicts

- **Deisenroth, Faisal and Ong, *Mathematics for Machine Learning* (organizer's pick) - start here.** Built precisely to take a first-year background up to ML math. This is your on-ramp for everything below.
- **Bishop, *Pattern Recognition and Machine Learning* - use targeted chapters.** The official book that best matches the Discord topic list and the sample's depth. Dense, but the sections mapped below are the canonical treatments.
- **Goodfellow, Bengio and Courville, *Deep Learning* - use Parts I-II ** The most readable of the three officials; an ideal middle ground.
- **Murphy, *Probabilistic Machine Learning* - reference only.** Free and encyclopedic, but far too big to study from in a month.

### The topic map

- **Foundation (roughly the first two weeks):** MML Ch. 2-4 (linear algebra, analytic geometry, matrix decompositions - eigendecomposition and the **SVD**), Ch. 5 (vector calculus - gradients, Jacobians, Hessians; section 5.6 is backpropagation), Ch. 6 (probability) and Ch. 7 (optimization - gradient descent, step sizes, **Lagrange multipliers and duality, convexity**). Watch 3Blue1Brown's *Essence of Linear Algebra* alongside for intuition.
- **Linear regression and regularization:** MML Ch. 9 (regression, with ridge as MAP estimation), Bishop Ch. 3 (regularized least squares), and Goodfellow sections 5.2-5.4 and 7.1 (the L1 and L2 penalties).

  Sample Problem 2 is exactly a ridge perturbation, solved with the derivative-of-a-matrix-inverse identity. Keep the **Matrix Cookbook** open as your lookup sheet for all matrix calculus.
- **SVMs:** MML Ch. 12 (margins, hinge loss, the dual, kernels) plus Bishop section 7.1. Know the margin geometry, the primal and dual forms, and what support vectors are.
- **Clustering:** Bishop section 9.1 (k-means: the objective and the update steps) and section 9.2 (Gaussian mixtures and EM at a conceptual level); MML Ch. 11 covers GMMs.
- **Neural networks:** Goodfellow Ch. 6 (feedforward nets; section 6.5 is backprop) plus Bishop sections 5.1-5.3. Be able to run backprop by hand on a two-layer network and to differentiate softmax composed with cross-entropy.
- **Information theory (the sample opens with it):** Cover and Thomas, *Elements of Information Theory*, **Ch. 2 only** - entropy, conditional entropy, mutual information, the chain rules, and the **data-processing inequality**. Sample Problem 1 is a one-line DPI argument once you know it. Free alternative: the early chapters of MacKay's *Information Theory, Inference, and Learning Algorithms*; quick versions in Goodfellow section 3.13 or Bishop section 1.6.
- **Loss functions (an official tag):** MSE, cross-entropy (and its information-theoretic reading), hinge. Know each one's gradient and which model it pairs with - you will cover them en route through the sections above.

### Practice

The official ML sample; the quizzes of Imperial's *Mathematics for Machine Learning* specialization on Coursera, audited free; the GATE DA paper plus GATE linear algebra and probability PYQs for speed; MML and Bishop end-of-chapter exercises for the listed sections; and hand-derivation drills - the OLS and ridge closed forms, the gradient of the logistic loss, one full backprop pass, and the entropy and mutual-information identities.

## Track 4 - Reinforcement Learning

*Official topics: Markov decision processes · policy and value functions · Bellman equations · dynamic programming · temporal-difference learning · exploration and exploitation.*

### Book verdicts

- **Sutton and Barto (2nd ed., free, organizer's pick) - the only text you need for Round 1.** Every official topic tag maps cleanly to a chapter.
- **Puterman, *Markov Decision Processes* - skip for now.** Graduate-level, measure-flavored rigor; relevant only if you reach the finale and need proof depth.
- **Bertsekas, *Dynamic Programming and Optimal Control* - skip for now.** Same reasoning - save it for the finale, if at all.

### Chapter map (Sutton and Barto)

- **Ch. 2 - multi-armed bandits.** This is the "Exploration and Exploitation" tag: $\varepsilon$-greedy, optimistic initialization, UCB.
- **Ch. 3 - finite MDPs.** The MDP tuple, returns and discounting, $V$ and $Q$, and the Bellman expectation and optimality equations.
- **Ch. 4 - dynamic programming.** Policy evaluation, policy iteration, value iteration - know exactly how they differ, and be able to run two iterations by hand.
- **Ch. 5-6 - Monte Carlo and TD learning.** The TD(0) update, SARSA vs Q-learning, on-policy vs off-policy.
- **Sections 13.1-13.3 - policy-gradient methods.** The REINFORCE identity.

### Lectures and drills

David Silver's UCL lectures **1-5** (introduction, MDPs, DP planning, model-free prediction, model-free control), **7** (policy gradient) and **9** (exploration and exploitation) - his slides are the best compressed revision sheets for this track. NPTEL's *Reinforcement Learning* course (Balaraman Ravindran, IIT Madras): do the weekly MCQ assignments for the MDP, DP and TD weeks - solutions are available, and the format matches Round 1 perfectly. The Coursera RL Specialization (University of Alberta) quizzes, audited free. And [OpenAI's Spinning Up](https://spinningup.openai.com/): the "Key Concepts" and "Intro to Policy Optimization" pages have the cleanest derivation of the log-derivative trick anywhere.

### Practice pattern

Build tiny two- or three-state MDPs, like sample Problem 1, and solve $V^*$ by hand. The workhorse identity: a reward of $r$ received forever is worth $\frac{r}{1-\gamma}$. Run value iteration two steps by hand. Do Sutton and Barto's short conceptual exercises for Ch. 2-6.

## The one-month plan (3-4 hours a day)

The daily rhythm: about an hour of new material, then 1.5-2 hours of timed solving to definite answers, then 30 minutes of flashcards and cheat-sheet building. Weight everything toward the tracks you have ranked highest. This plan is built for the final month before Round 1 - if you are starting earlier, stretch weeks 2 and 3.

- **Week 1 - foundations.** MML Ch. 2-6 at speed, with 3Blue1Brown alongside; Rosen Ch. 1, 3 and 6; Sutton and Barto Ch. 2-3. Start all four cheat sheets. And attempt the four sample papers cold, once, early in the week - not to score well, but to learn exactly where the gap is.
- **Week 2 - the discrete and number theory spine.** Rosen Ch. 7-8 and 10-11 with heavy exercise drilling; Burton or NZM from divisibility through Fermat-Euler, with Apostol Ch. 1, 2 and 5 in parallel; MML Ch. 7 and the start of Ch. 9. Practice: TOMATO and ISI UGA sets for both tracks, plus Project Euler number theory.
- **Week 3 - ML and RL depth, number theory finish.** The targeted Bishop and Goodfellow sections in order: regression and regularization → SVMs → clustering → neural nets and backprop → information theory via Cover and Thomas Ch. 2. Sutton and Barto Ch. 4-6 plus sections 13.1-13.3, with Silver's lectures 3-5, 7 and 9 and the NPTEL assignments. Number theory: primitive roots, quadratic residues, the Diophantine chapter, the crypto chapter.
- **Week 4 - mocks and repair.** Re-sit all four sample papers, timed, solving everything to a definite answer. Mixed timed sets from ISI, CMI and Putnam-style objective problems, plus the NPTEL and Coursera quizzes. Grind the error log. Daily flashcard sprints across all four cheat sheets. Taper on the last day.
