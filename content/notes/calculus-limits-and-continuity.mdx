These are the definitions and results you need cold before anything else in
analysis makes sense. Keep this open next to your problem set.

## The definition

We say $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ when $f(x)$ can be forced as close to $L$ as
we like by taking $x$ close enough to $a$:

$$
\forall \varepsilon > 0 \;\; \exists \delta > 0 : \quad
0 < |x - a| < \delta \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon
$$

Two things students miss on first reading:

- The $0 <$ in $0 < |x - a|$ means the value $f(a)$ itself is **irrelevant** -
  a limit is about the approach, not the arrival.
- The order of quantifiers matters: $\varepsilon$ is the adversary's challenge,
  $\delta$ is your answer. Your $\delta$ may depend on $\varepsilon$; never the
  other way around.

## A worked epsilon-delta proof

**Claim.** $\lim_{x \to 3} (2x + 1) = 7$.

**Proof.** Let $\varepsilon > 0$. Choose $\delta = \varepsilon / 2$. If
$0 < |x - 3| < \delta$, then

$$
|(2x + 1) - 7| = |2x - 6| = 2\,|x - 3| < 2\delta = \varepsilon.
$$

That's the whole game: work backwards from $|f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$ until you
can read off a sufficient $\delta$.

## The limit laws

Once a few basic limits exist, arithmetic gives you the rest. If
$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ and $\lim_{x \to a} g(x) = M$:

| Law | Statement |
| --- | --- |
| Sum | $\lim\,(f + g) = L + M$ |
| Product | $\lim\,(fg) = LM$ |
| Quotient | $\lim\,(f/g) = L/M$, provided $M \neq 0$ |
| Squeeze | if $f \le h \le g$ near $a$ and $L = M$, then $\lim h = L$ |

The squeeze theorem is the one that earns its keep - it's how you prove the
famous $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1$.

## Continuity, and the three ways it fails

$f$ is **continuous at $a$** when $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$ - the limit
exists, the value exists, and they agree. Each ingredient can fail on its own:

1. **Removable** - the limit exists but disagrees with (or lacks) a value:
   $f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1}$ at $x = 1$. Patchable by redefining one point.
2. **Jump** - one-sided limits exist but differ: the step function at $0$.
3. **Essential** - a one-sided limit doesn't exist at all:
   $f(x) = \sin(1/x)$ at $0$, which oscillates forever.

## The two theorems that do the heavy lifting

For $f$ continuous on a closed interval $[a, b]$:

- **Intermediate Value Theorem.** $f$ attains every value between $f(a)$ and
  $f(b)$. This is how you prove equations have roots without solving them.
- **Extreme Value Theorem.** $f$ attains a maximum and a minimum on $[a, b]$.
  Both the "closed" and the "bounded" in $[a, b]$ are load-bearing - drop
  either and the theorem dies (try $f(x) = 1/x$ on $(0, 1]$).
